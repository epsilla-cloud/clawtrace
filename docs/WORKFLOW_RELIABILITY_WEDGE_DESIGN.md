## Problem Statement
OpenClaw is being used for real company workflows, but its behavior feels like a black box in two different ways.

First, individual runs fail and operators cannot quickly tell what happened, where the workflow broke, what changed, or what to fix before the next run.

Second, the agent drifts over time. Important rules live partly in `AGENTS.md`, partly in `MEMORY.md`, partly in chat, and partly in habits the operator never automated. After compaction or repeated edits, the agent can end up following contradictory instructions, forgetting rules that were never persisted, or repeatedly making the same mistake for reasons that are invisible in normal logs.

The immediate pain is not abstract "observability." It is daily business interruption and operator babysitting.

The internal example is a daily SEO publishing workflow that generates a cover image, pulls news, synthesizes content, applies SEO skills, commits code, pushes, and deploys to Vercel. Because the workflow is unreliable, the CEO still has to manually inspect output and re-prompt the agent every day.

## Demand Evidence
- Founder demand is real. The SEO workflow steals roughly 30 minutes of CEO time every day.
- The internal failure pattern is sharp, not vague: repeated mistakes, especially around the cover image generation step, with failure rate still above 50 percent even after repeated attempts to let the agent self-correct.
- Community pain appears real across two different threads:
  - Thread 1 surfaced runtime-debugging pain and messy workarounds: gateway logs, Claude Code / Cursor / Warp / Grok as a second debugging agent, custom scripts, backups, plugins, and proxies.
  - Thread 2 surfaced control-plane drift pain: contradictory instructions across `AGENTS.md` and `MEMORY.md`, important rules left only in chat and lost after compaction, repetitive manual routines that should have been automated, and ad hoc audits to realign the agent.
- The second thread matters because it shows the problem is not just "why did this run fail?" It is also "why has my agent gotten weird over time?"
- What is still missing: evidence that an external user will pay or pilot immediately. Tomorrow's design-partner conversation with Acme is important because it upgrades a plausible customer into a real one or disproves the wedge.

## Status Quo
Today the workaround is duct tape:
- babysit the run manually
- inspect gateway logs
- ask OpenClaw to fix itself
- bring in a stronger second agent such as Claude Code, Cursor, Warp AI, Grok, or a custom script to inspect logs and propose changes
- rerun or partially rerun the workflow
- keep manual backups of config or state before major changes
- run context or file audits to see what instructions are actually loaded
- move important rules from chat into `AGENTS.md` or related files so they survive compaction
- use tools or skills that scan for contradictions between files
- give deterministic work to scripts or cron jobs instead of trusting the agent to do it consistently

The cost of the workaround internally is clear: about 30 minutes of founder time every day. The cost externally is directionally supported by community responses, but still needs quantification in buyer interviews.

## Constraints
- Pre-product: no external users yet
- Strong internal pain, weaker external proof
- The broad "Datadog for AI agents" vision is compelling but too wide as an initial wedge
- A pure trace viewer is not enough if many failures come from state drift rather than runtime execution alone
- The product must know when to say: this should be a script or cron job, not a more agentic workflow
- OpenClaw is the right initial community wedge, but the company should not trap itself into being OpenClaw-only long term
- The first buyer likely values saved operator time and restored control more than sophisticated platform breadth

## Premises
1. The initial ICP is not all agent builders. It is founder/operators and small technical teams running one revenue-adjacent OpenClaw workflow they still have to babysit.
2. The first paid wedge should be a workflow reliability debugger with run replay, state diff, contradiction detection, and fix recommendation, not a full multi-framework observability platform.
3. The real launch competitor is logs plus Claude Code / Cursor plus scripts plus reruns plus backups plus drift-audit skills/plugins, not Datadog or LangSmith.

## Approaches Considered
### Approach A: Workflow Reliability Debugger
Start with one critical OpenClaw workflow and make failures explainable end to end: what happened, where it broke, what changed, whether the likely cause is runtime or drift, and what to fix next.

Pros:
- strongest match to current pain
- clearest ROI story
- easiest first sale
- sharpest differentiation against the actual status quo
- captures both daily failure debugging and the beginning of drift diagnosis

Cons:
- narrower story
- risks looking like a debugging tool rather than a platform company

### Approach B: Agent Reliability Control Plane
Launch the full vision: chat-first observability, traces, dashboards, alerts, evals, feedback, sessions, drift auditing, and multi-agent health across workflows.

Pros:
- matches the long-term company vision
- best enterprise expansion surface
- strongest architecture coherence

Cons:
- too broad for the first buyer
- harder to explain what gets purchased first
- easier to blur into the existing AI observability category

### Approach C: Agent Trainer / Reliability Autopilot
Focus on repeated failures and self-healing: nightly analysis, repeated-mistake detection, guarded fix suggestions, drift audits, regression checks, and fewer repeated mistakes over time.

Pros:
- memorable and emotionally compelling
- closer to the real user desire to stop babysitting agents
- naturally incorporates both runtime failures and drift cleanup

Cons:
- harder to trust early
- depends on strong observability underneath
- higher product and safety complexity in v1

## Recommended Approach
Hybrid of A and B.

Translation:
- sell Approach A
- build toward Approach B

The first product should be a workflow reliability debugger for one flaky OpenClaw workflow, but it must include enough drift detection to diagnose contradictions across files and lost instructions when those are the real cause. The company story can still be "the control plane for agent reliability," but the first thing someone buys should be much smaller and more urgent: explain today's failed workflow, tell me what changed, and tell me whether I should fix the agent, fix the instructions, or just script this step.

Suggested v1 surface:
- one workflow dashboard
- run story / trace explanation
- state diff versus last-known-good
- contradiction detector across `AGENTS.md`, `MEMORY.md`, and persistent instruction files
- suggested next fix, labeled as one of:
  - runtime failure
  - state drift
  - should be scripted
- guardrail or alert creation from the incident
- promotion of repeated failures into regression tests

Cost scope (must be first-class in the same flow):
- show token and estimated cost at four levels: portfolio, workflow, trajectory, and step class
- answer "where did cost go?" using explicit breakdowns:
  - by workflow
  - by model
  - by repeated retry loops
  - by high-risk mutating steps
- include cost-per-success as a core KPI next to success rate and MTTR
- generate next-best-actions that reduce cost without silently reducing reliability
  - cap repeated retries
  - add budget guardrails per step/workflow
  - recommend script promotion for deterministic high-cost steps
- keep this integrated into onboarding and control-room views, not as a separate "finance" page

Why this is better than a normal observability launch:
- it solves the founder's daily workflow pain immediately
- it absorbs the strongest lesson from the second thread: many users do not just need more traces, they need help untangling their own contradictory agent state

## Success Criteria
- Reduce daily operator babysitting on a target workflow from roughly 30 minutes to under 5 minutes.
- Let a user understand a failed workflow in under 60 seconds.
- Surface a likely cause and a next action for the majority of high-friction failures.
- Correctly distinguish runtime failures from drift-related failures often enough that users trust the diagnosis.
- Convert repeated failures into reusable regression assets.
- Get at least 3 to 5 design partners who each have one critical workflow they actively monitor with ClawTrace.
- Get at least one external design partner to say some version of: "If you can reliably do this for my workflow, I would pilot or pay."

## The Assignment
Tomorrow, do not pitch "Datadog for AI agents."
Pitch this instead:
- "If your daily OpenClaw workflow fails tomorrow morning, would you pay for a tool that shows exactly what happened, what changed, whether the issue is bad runtime behavior or drift in your instructions, and what to fix before the next run?"

Ask the Acme design partner six concrete questions:
1. Which workflow would you trust first?
2. What failure hurts most today?
3. When behavior feels off, is it usually a bad run or a slow drift in instructions and config?
4. What output would be enough for you to act immediately: explanation, state diff, contradiction detector, recommended fix, or alert?
5. Which tasks should really just be scripts or cron jobs instead of agent steps?
6. If this worked, would you pilot it, pay for it, or just find it interesting?

The goal of the interview is not validation theater. It is to find the first paid wedge or kill the wrong one quickly.
